Module 4

The Mesh by Kwesi Brew

Identifying devices and linking them to meaning

1. Module Overview

This module focuses on literary and poetic devices—tools writers use to shape meaning, sound, and emotional impact. Using Kwesi Brew’s poem “The Mesh” as a case study, learners identify devices such as imagery, metaphor, symbolism, alliteration, assonance, and enjambment, and explain how these devices support themes of love, ambiguity, and decision-making.

2. Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, learners should be able to:

  1. Define key literary devices (imagery, metaphor, symbolism, alliteration, assonance, enjambment).
  2. Identify these devices in a short poem.
  3. Explain how devices contribute to theme and tone.
  4. Write a short analysis of a poem using evidence from the text.

3. Module Content

Poem Text: “The Mesh” (Kwesi Brew / Osborne Henry)

We have come to the cross-roads
And I must either leave or come with you.
I lingered over the choice
But in the darkness of my doubts
You lifted the lamp of love
And I saw in your face
The road that I should take.

Summary and Themes

The speaker describes reaching a critical moment—standing at a “cross-roads” and needing to choose. In uncertainty (“darkness of my doubts”), the presence of love becomes a guiding light, helping the speaker see the path to take.

Major themes:

  • Love: Love is presented as guidance and clarity.
  • Decision-making: The poem centres on choice at a turning point.
  • Ambiguity: The poem leaves room for interpretation about the relationship and the chosen road.

Devices in “The Mesh” (Selected)

  • Assonance: Repetition of vowel sounds (e.g., “either leave or come with you”).
  • Alliteration: Repetition of initial consonant sounds (e.g., “come to the cross-roads”; “darkness of my doubts”).
  • Enjambment: Meaning runs beyond line breaks (e.g., “You lifted the lamp of love / And I saw in your face…”).
  • Imagery: Concrete pictures such as “cross-roads,” “darkness,” “lamp,” and “road.”
  • Metaphor: “Lamp of love” and “road” suggest guidance and life direction; “darkness of my doubts” compares uncertainty to darkness.
  • Symbolism: The lamp and road symbolise choice and direction in life.
  • Free verse: The poem does not follow a strict rhyme scheme.

Key Quotation

“You lifted the lamp of love
And I saw in your face
The road that I should take.”

4. Classroom Examples

  • Example 1: Explain what “cross-roads” symbolises in the poem.
  • Example 2: Identify one metaphor in the poem and explain what it means.
  • Example 3: Find one sound device (assonance or alliteration) and show the words that create it.

5. Interactive Learning Activities

  • Activity 1: Device Mark-up
    Copy the poem into your notebook and underline: 1 image, 1 symbol, and 1 example of sound pattern.
  • Activity 2: Meaning Link
    In groups, explain how “darkness” and “lamp” work together to show the speaker’s situation.
  • Activity 3: One-Paragraph Analysis
    Write 6–8 sentences explaining the choice the speaker faces and how at least two devices support the theme.

6. Reflection Questions

  • Why do writers use symbolism instead of stating ideas directly?
  • How does the poem’s simplicity strengthen its message?
  • What other life situations could “cross-roads” represent?

7. Application Task

Write a short poem (7–12 lines) about a decision you once faced. Include at least:

  • one metaphor,
  • one symbol,
  • and one sound device (alliteration or assonance).
