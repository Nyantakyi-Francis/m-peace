Module 5

A Man of the People (Chinua Achebe)

Summary, setting, characters, and themes

1. Module Overview

This module studies A Man of the People by Chinua Achebe as a satirical novel that critiques post-independence African leadership. Learners examine how the novel exposes corruption, hypocrisy, tribalism, and the victimisation of opponents, and how these problems affect ordinary citizens.

2. Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, learners should be able to:

  1. Describe the genre and purpose of satire in the novel.
  2. Summarise the plot and identify key conflicts.
  3. Explain how setting (rural and urban) shapes political behaviour and social life.
  4. Describe major characters and their roles in the conflict.
  5. Discuss key themes such as corruption, hypocrisy, and oppression.

3. Module Content

Overview and Summary

  • Author: Chinua Achebe
  • Publication Year: 1966
  • Genre: Satirical Novel
  • Core Subject: A critique of post-independence leadership and the misuse of public office for private gain.

The story is narrated by Odili Samalu, a young educated school teacher in an unnamed modern African nation representing post-colonial Nigeria. Odili’s conflict is with his former teacher, Chief Nanga, now the powerful and corrupt Minister of Culture. Their rivalry grows from Nanga’s corruption and his affair with Odili’s girlfriend, Elsie. Odili later joins the Common People’s Convention (CPC) to contest elections. The story ends in a chaotic election period and a military coup d’état.

Setting and Context

Setting: Post-colonial Nigeria/Africa during transition from colonial rule to self-governance.

  • Rural setting (Anata, Urua): Limited social services (no electricity, no pipe-borne water), high illiteracy and ignorance, making citizens easy targets for exploitation.
  • Urban setting (Giligili, Bori, Warri): Affluent homes, electricity, running water, and access to media (newspapers, television), highlighting inequality and political privilege.

Character Profiles

  • Chief Nanga: Minister of Culture, POP leader; corrupt, hypocritical, tribalistic, womaniser; uses public funds and bribery to win power.
  • Odili Samalu: Narrator/protagonist; educated and critical of corruption; joins CPC and contests against Nanga.
  • Maxwell Kulamu: Lawyer/poet and CPC co-founder; idealistic; assassinated after refusing a bribe.
  • Chief Simon Koko: Minister of Overseas Training; highly corrupt; involved in Maxwell’s murder; later killed by Eunice.
  • Edna Odo: Educated and sympathetic; victim of poverty-driven arranged marriage; later marries Odili after Nanga’s arrest.
  • Eunice: Maxwell’s fiancée and CPC co-founder; courageous; avenges Maxwell by shooting Chief Koko.
  • Hezekiah Samalu: Odili’s father; local POP chairman; polygamist; former district interpreter.
  • Elsie: Odili’s unfaithful girlfriend; cheats with Chief Nanga.

Major Themes

  • Corruption: Public office used for private gain (wealth, luxury, bribery, misappropriation of funds).
  • Irresponsibility: Leaders and citizens fail to act responsibly, slowing development and neglecting public projects.
  • Ignorance: Rural masses are easily manipulated with small gifts and false promises.
  • Betrayal: Politicians betray voters and the ideals of independence.
  • Hypocrisy: Leaders preach virtue while practicing vice.
  • Nepotism and tribalism: Loyalty to tribe/town influences appointments and distribution of benefits.
  • Victimization and oppression: Violence, intimidation, and arrests used against opponents.
  • Embezzlement of public funds: National resources drained while citizens remain poor.

4. Interactive Learning Activities

  • Activity 1: Create a character map showing how Odili, Nanga, Edna, Elsie, Maxwell, Koko, and Eunice connect.
  • Activity 2: In groups, list examples of corruption from the summary and explain their effects on ordinary people.
  • Activity 3: Debate: Are citizens partly responsible for corrupt leadership? Give reasons.

5. Reflection Questions

  • Why does Achebe use satire to present politics and leadership?
  • How does the rural–urban contrast help the reader understand inequality?
  • Which theme do you think is most relevant today, and why?

6. Application Task

Essay (1–2 pages): “Corruption and Its Consequences in A Man of the People.”

In your response, discuss:

  • how Chief Nanga maintains power,
  • how citizens and institutions respond,
  • the outcomes of political oppression and violence.
