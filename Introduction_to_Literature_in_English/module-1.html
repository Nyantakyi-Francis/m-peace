Module 1

Introduction to Literature in English

Understanding literature, its forms, genres, and key terms

1. Module Overview

This module introduces learners to literature in English by explaining what literature is, the major types of literature (oral and written), and the major genres (poetry, drama, and prose). It also introduces key features, functions, and elements of each genre, and concludes with common literary devices and terms used in analysis.

2. Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, learners should be able to:

  1. Define literature and explain its value.
  2. Distinguish between oral literature and written literature.
  3. Identify the major genres of literature: poetry, drama, and prose.
  4. Explain the nature, functions, types, and elements of poetry.
  5. Explain the nature, functions, forms/types, and elements of drama.
  6. Explain the nature of prose and describe major prose forms (especially the novel).
  7. Identify and use common literary devices and terms in basic analysis.

3. Module Content

Definition of Literature

Literature comprises written, and historically oral, works that exhibit artistic or intellectual value. It functions as a medium to explore the human condition, cultural values, and societal structures.

Types of Literature

  • Oral Literature: Transmitted by word of mouth across generations (includes folktales, myths, legends, and oral poetry).
  • Written Literature: Recorded texts designed for preservation and critical analysis.

Genres of Literature

The major divisions include poetry, drama, and prose, each employing distinct structural and aesthetic conventions.

Poetry

Nature of Poetry: A concentrated form of literary expression that relies heavily on rhythm, figurative language, and condensed imagery. It privileges the musical and emotive qualities of language.

Function of Poetry:

  • Expresses deep psychological, emotional, and social realities.
  • Provides aesthetic pleasure while challenging the readerâ€™s perception of language and meaning.

Types of Poetry:

  • Lyric: Short, subjective poems expressing personal emotions and thoughts, usually written in the first person.
  • Narrative: Poetry that tells a story, such as epics and ballads.
  • Dramatic: Poetry written in the voice of a character or characters, intended for performance or dramatic effect.

Elements of Poetry:

  • Stanzaic Structure: The organization of lines into groups.
  • Rhythm and Meter: The structural cadence and metrical foot patterns.
  • Imagery: Sensory details used to create mental pictures.
  • Figurative Language: Metaphor, simile, personification, and symbolism.
  • Theme and Tone: The central idea and the speakerâ€™s attitude toward the subject matter.

Drama

Nature of Drama: A performance-oriented literary genre written in dialogue and action. It bridges the written text and the live theatrical experience.

Function of Drama:

  • Engages audiences directly through visual and auditory realisation.
  • Serves as a platform for public discourse, catharsis, and critique of societal norms.

Forms of Drama:

  • Tragedy: Serious action involving the downfall of a noble protagonist, leading to catharsis.
  • Comedy: Lighter works dealing with human folly or social conflicts, usually ending in resolution or harmony.
  • Verse Drama: Plays written primarily in poetic meter (e.g., blank verse or rhymed verse).

Types of Drama: Includes tragicomedy, melodrama, and farce.

Elements of Drama (adapted from classic dramatic theory):

  • Plot: The causal arrangement of incidents.
  • Character: The psychological and social agents who drive the narrative.
  • Thought/Theme: The underlying philosophical or social message.
  • Diction: The linguistic style and dialogue choices.
  • Spectacle and Music: The visual and auditory elements of the stage production.

Prose

Nature of Prose: Ordinary writing that mimics the natural flow of spoken language. It is organised into sentences and paragraphs rather than stanzas or metrical lines.

The Novel: An extended narrative that allows for complex plot development, detailed settings, and deep psychological exploration of characters.

Function of the Novel: Captures the social, historical, and individual dynamics of a culture.

Forms of the Novel:

  • Epistolary: Composed of letters or diary entries.
  • Picaresque: Follows the episodic adventures of a rogue or underdog.
  • Other Forms: Historical, sociological, gothic, and psychological fiction.

Types of Prose: Includes fiction (novels, short stories) and nonfiction (essays, biographies, critical treatises).

Literary Devices and Terms

Figurative Devices: Metaphor, simile, personification, hyperbole, and irony.

Sound Devices: Alliteration, assonance, consonance, and onomatopoeia.

Structural and Thematic Devices: Motif, symbolism, paradox, juxtaposition, and foreshadowing.

4. Classroom Examples

  • Example 1: Identify the genre (poetry, drama, or prose) and mention two features that justify your answer.
  • Example 2: In a short poem, point out one image and one figurative device (e.g. a metaphor or simile) and explain the effect.
  • Example 3: In a short dramatic excerpt, identify the conflict in the plot and the character(s) involved.

5. Interactive Learning Activities

  • Activity 1: Quick Definitions
    In your own words, define literature and list two reasons why people study it.
  • Activity 2: Oral vs Written
    Work in pairs to list three examples of oral literature and three examples of written literature.
  • Activity 3: Genre Sorting
    In groups, sort the following into the correct genre: Poetry, Drama, or Prose:
    • A play performed on stage.
    • A novel or short story.
    • A lyric poem.
    • A tragedy.
  • Activity 4: Devices Hunt
    Pick a short passage from a poem or story and identify any two devices (e.g. metaphor, irony, alliteration, symbolism). Explain what each device adds to meaning.

6. Reflection Questions

  • How is oral literature different from written literature?
  • What makes poetry different from prose?
  • Why is drama described as a performance-oriented genre?
  • Which literary devices do you find easiest or most difficult to identify, and why?
