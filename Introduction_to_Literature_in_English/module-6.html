Module 6

The Blinkards (Kobina Sekyi)

Summary, setting, themes, characters, and dramatic techniques

1. Module Overview

This module studies The Blinkards by Kobina Sekyi as a satirical play that criticises blind imitation of foreign (European) culture in the Gold Coast. Learners examine how the play exposes cultural confusion, hypocrisy, and class divisions, and how it defends indigenous values and customary practices.

2. Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, learners should be able to:

  1. Explain the historical background and colonial context of the play.
  2. Describe the setting and how it supports the satire.
  3. Discuss major themes such as blind imitation, colonial indoctrination, and tradition vs. modernity.
  4. Describe key characters and what they represent.
  5. Identify dramatic techniques (satire, comedy, contrast, diction) used to create meaning.

3. Module Content

Overview and Background

  • Author: Kobina Sekyi (William Ekow Sekyi)
  • Writing Year: 1915 (colonial era)
  • First Publication: 1974
  • Genre: Satirical play
  • Context: Set in the Gold Coast (modern Ghana) during a period when some educated elites began to idolise European education and culture while despising indigenous heritage.

Setting

  • Location: Cape Coast, Gold Coast (modern-day Ghana).
  • Environment: The play contrasts westernised, English-imitating elites (e.g., the Cosmopolitan Club) with indigenous cultural practices.
  • Significance: Highlights cultural confusion, class divisions, and the clash between traditional African values and European Victorian-era mannerisms.

Major Themes

  • Blind imitation of foreign culture: Characters copy British dress, food, and mannerisms without understanding the reasons behind them.
  • Colonialism and indoctrination: Colonial schools/churches create divisions that make some people label indigenous practices as “uncivilized”.
  • Clash between tradition and modernity: Contrasts European household roles with traditional African family structures.
  • Marriage and customary laws: Conflict around the marriage ordinance of 1884 vs. native customary law (family consent, procedures), and how imitation of European style fails.
  • Hypocrisy and false intellect: Characters use faulty grammar and shallow “sophistication” while pretending to be refined.

Character Profiles

  • Mr. Onyimdze: British-trained lawyer proud of indigenous culture; voice of reason; defends customary marriage laws in court.
  • Mrs. Brofosem: “Leader of fashion”; domineering, extravagant, blind imitator of European culture; later realises the emptiness of imitation.
  • Mr. Brofosem: Submissive husband and comic foil; later rebels against financial strain and returns to native traditions.
  • Mr. Okadu: Young aspiring educated man; impressionable and eager to conform to English mannerisms.
  • Miss Tsiba: Daughter of Mr. Tsiba; educated by Mrs. Brofosem; caught in forced/arranged marriage and its consequences.

Style and Dramatic Techniques

  • Diction: Satire is reinforced through incorrect grammar among “English imitators”, while culturally grounded characters use clearer, correct English.
  • Character naming (carinyms): Names reflect personalities/behaviours (e.g., Brofosem and “Englishness”).
  • Comedy and satire: Ridicules awkward adoption of foreign habits for humour and critique.
  • Contrast and suspense: Side-by-side contrasts (e.g., Mr. Onyimdze vs. Mrs. Brofosem) highlight the play’s message.

4. Interactive Learning Activities

  • Activity 1: List three ways the elites imitate European culture and explain why the play criticises each one.
  • Activity 2: In groups, compare Mr. Onyimdze and Mrs. : what values does each represent?
  • Activity 3: Short drama task: act a brief scene that shows “blind imitation” and discuss its message.

5. Reflection Questions

  • Why do you think some people reject their own culture after receiving foreign education?
  • How does satire make criticism more effective than direct preaching?
  • What lessons from The Blinkards are relevant today?

6. Application Task

Essay (1–2 pages): “Blind Imitation and Cultural Identity in The Blinkards.”

In your response, discuss:

  • two characters who represent imitation and two who represent cultural pride,
  • how comedy/satire is used to criticise society,
  • one lesson the play teaches about cultural identity.
