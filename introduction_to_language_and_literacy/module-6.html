Module 6

The Language/Literacy Teacher

A Six-Module Professional Learning Course by Miss Mprah Ruhaimatu

1. Module Overview

This module focuses on the teacher as a central figure in language and literacy development. It introduces student teachers to the qualities, responsibilities, attitudes, and professional practices that make someone an effective language or literacy teacher. It also encourages future teachers to think critically about how they can grow into reflective, inclusive, competent, and learner-centred professionals.

Language and literacy development do not happen automatically in the classroom. They require a teacher who understands learners, uses language purposefully, selects appropriate methods, encourages participation, and responds to learner differences with patience and skill. In Ghanaian classrooms, where learners often come from diverse linguistic, social, and educational backgrounds, the role of the language/literacy teacher becomes even more important.

The module therefore explores the characteristics of a good literacy teacher, the broader role of the language/literacy teacher, the steps involved in becoming a good literacy teacher, and the value of interaction with small groups of learners through observation and reflective practice.

2. Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, student teachers should be able to:

  1. Identify and explain the characteristics of a good literacy teacher.
  2. Discuss the role of the language/literacy teacher in the classroom.
  3. Explain how teachers can support literacy development among diverse learners.
  4. Reflect on the challenges of literacy development and how they can be addressed.
  5. Discuss how school observation and small-group interaction can improve professional understanding.
  6. Identify personal steps they can take to become effective language and literacy teachers.
  7. Demonstrate a reflective understanding of what professional literacy teaching requires in Ghana.

3. Module Content

Who is a Language/Literacy Teacher?

A language/literacy teacher is a teacher who helps learners develop the ability to communicate, understand, interpret, and use language effectively for speaking, listening, reading, writing, and meaning-making. In a broad sense, every teacher has a literacy role because all subjects depend on language for explanation, comprehension, response, and assessment.

However, a language/literacy teacher is not only someone who teaches language content. It is also a teacher who supports learners to become more confident users of language across school life. Such a teacher recognises that literacy is foundational to academic success and social participation.

Why the Language/Literacy Teacher Matters

Learners’ success in school is strongly influenced by the quality of teaching they receive. A good language/literacy teacher does not only transmit knowledge but also creates opportunities for learners to think, talk, question, read, write, and grow in confidence. Where literacy teaching is weak, learners may struggle not only in language lessons but across all subjects.

In Ghana, many learners face literacy challenges due to factors such as multilingual classrooms, limited reading culture, inadequate learning resources, and uneven home support. This means the teacher must be knowledgeable, reflective, flexible, and committed to learner development.

Characteristics of a Good Literacy Teacher

A good literacy teacher possesses both professional knowledge and human qualities. Effective literacy teaching is not based on content knowledge alone. It also depends on attitude, responsiveness, planning, and the ability to connect with learners.

Some important characteristics of a good literacy teacher include:

  • Strong language competence: the teacher uses language clearly, correctly, and meaningfully.
  • Patience: the teacher understands that learners develop at different rates.
  • Inclusiveness: the teacher respects learner diversity and supports all learners.
  • Good communication skills: the teacher explains clearly and listens actively.
  • Creativity: the teacher uses interesting and varied strategies to support literacy.
  • Reflectiveness: the teacher thinks critically about teaching practice and learner response.
  • Commitment: the teacher is dedicated to learner growth and continuous improvement.
  • Empathy: the teacher understands learner struggles and responds with encouragement.
  • Professional preparedness: the teacher plans lessons and uses suitable resources.

The Role of the Language/Literacy Teacher

The role of the language/literacy teacher is broad. The teacher is not only an instructor but also a facilitator, motivator, guide, assessor, model, and supporter of learning. Good literacy teaching involves helping learners to understand language, use it confidently, and apply it meaningfully in different contexts.

The teacher’s role includes:

  • teaching language and literacy skills clearly and systematically,
  • modelling good speaking, listening, reading, and writing behaviours,
  • creating a language-rich classroom environment,
  • encouraging classroom participation and confidence,
  • identifying learners who need extra support,
  • using inclusive methods for diverse learners,
  • assessing progress and giving constructive feedback,
  • promoting positive attitudes toward reading and language use.

The Teacher as a Model of Language Use

Learners often learn language not only from formal lessons but also from how teachers speak, explain, question, and respond in the classroom. This means the teacher is a live model of language use. A careless or discouraging language example from the teacher may affect learners negatively, while clear and respectful communication can improve learner confidence and understanding.

A good literacy teacher therefore models:

  • clear pronunciation,
  • respectful communication,
  • good listening habits,
  • thoughtful questioning,
  • purposeful reading and writing behaviours.

The Teacher and Diverse Learners

A good language/literacy teacher understands that learners differ in language background, confidence level, learning pace, home support, and literacy experience. Some may be strong oral communicators but weak readers. Others may understand ideas but struggle to express them in English. Some may be shy, while others may be active but inaccurate.

For this reason, the teacher must not teach as though all learners are the same. Effective literacy teaching requires differentiation, encouragement, patience, and flexible support for all categories of learners.

Challenges to Literacy Development among Learners

To become a good literacy teacher, student teachers must understand the real difficulties learners face. Literacy challenges may arise from several sources, including the home, school environment, teaching methods, and learner circumstances.

Common challenges include:

  • poor reading culture,
  • limited access to books and print materials,
  • multilingual classroom complexity,
  • negative attitudes toward reading or local languages,
  • learner fear, shyness, or low confidence,
  • inadequate support for struggling learners,
  • large class size and limited individual attention,
  • weak home literacy support.

Addressing Literacy Challenges as a Teacher

Good literacy teachers do not merely complain about challenges; they look for practical ways to address them. Even in difficult conditions, a reflective and creative teacher can make a difference.

Ways of addressing literacy challenges include:

  • creating a supportive and encouraging classroom atmosphere,
  • using group work and pair work to increase participation,
  • building reading habits through regular reading opportunities,
  • using storytelling, discussion, questioning, and interactive tasks,
  • scaffolding tasks for weaker learners,
  • using locally available materials creatively,
  • respecting learners’ linguistic backgrounds,
  • working collaboratively with colleagues, mentors, and schools.

Becoming a Good Literacy Teacher

Becoming a good literacy teacher is a process. It does not happen automatically simply because one has entered teacher education. It requires intentional growth, self-discipline, practice, humility, and reflection.

Student teachers can become better literacy teachers by:

  • reading widely about language and literacy education,
  • observing experienced teachers carefully,
  • practising clear and effective communication,
  • developing positive attitudes toward all learners,
  • learning to plan lessons well,
  • reflecting honestly on their strengths and weaknesses,
  • welcoming feedback from mentors and peers,
  • continuously improving their knowledge and methods.

Observation and Field Experience

Observation is an important part of becoming a professional teacher. Through field work and school visits, student teachers see how literacy is promoted in real classrooms. They observe how teachers explain concepts, respond to learner difficulties, manage participation, and use resources in actual teaching situations.

Observation helps student teachers move from theory to practice. It also allows them to identify both effective strategies and areas where classroom literacy development needs improvement.

Interaction with Small Groups of Learners

Working with small groups of learners is especially valuable because it gives student teachers the opportunity to observe learners more closely and respond to their needs more directly. In small groups, teachers can listen to how learners speak, notice reading or comprehension difficulties, encourage shy pupils, and provide more personalised support.

Such interaction also helps student teachers understand that literacy development is not abstract. It is experienced differently by individual learners, and good teaching often depends on careful attention to those differences.

The Teacher as a Reflective Practitioner

Reflective practice is one of the most important qualities of a good teacher. A reflective teacher thinks seriously about what happened in the lesson, what worked, what did not work, why learners responded in particular ways, and what can be improved next time.

Reflection helps teachers avoid routine teaching. It encourages growth, deeper understanding, and more responsive practice. In literacy teaching, where learners often differ greatly in background and ability, reflection is essential.

Professional Responsibility in Ghanaian Classrooms

In the Ghanaian context, the language/literacy teacher must be especially sensitive to issues such as multilingualism, varying learner confidence in English, differences in exposure to print, and community attitudes toward language use. Good teaching requires not only theoretical understanding but also sensitivity to local realities.

A professional teacher in Ghana must therefore combine strong content knowledge with cultural awareness, inclusive practice, and a genuine commitment to helping all learners develop language and literacy skills.

Why This Module Matters

This module matters because no curriculum, theory, or policy can succeed without a capable teacher. The language/literacy teacher is the one who turns ideas into practice, creates opportunities for learning, and supports learners in real classrooms.

By reflecting on the characteristics of a good literacy teacher and the demands of the profession, student teachers are better prepared to develop into thoughtful, inclusive, and effective educators.

4. Ghanaian Classroom Examples

  • Example 1: A teacher notices that some pupils rarely volunteer answers in English, but they speak confidently in pair work. Instead of labelling them weak, the teacher uses smaller discussion groups to build their confidence gradually.
  • Example 2: In a school with few reading materials, a creative teacher uses story retelling, word cards, locally made charts, and oral discussion to keep literacy learning active.
  • Example 3: During school observation, a student teacher realises that one teacher dominates classroom talk while learners remain passive. This helps the student teacher reflect on the importance of participation and interaction in literacy development.
  • Example 4: A teacher working with a small group notices that one learner can read aloud but does not understand what was read. This helps the teacher see that literacy support must go beyond pronunciation to include comprehension.

5. Interactive Learning Activities

  • Activity 1: Self-Assessment Checklist
    Write down five qualities of a good literacy teacher and assess yourself honestly against each one. Which qualities do you already show, and which ones do you still need to develop?
  • Activity 2: Group Discussion
    In groups, discuss this statement: “Every teacher is a literacy teacher.” Give examples from different subjects to support your view.
  • Activity 3: Observation Task
    During a school visit or teaching practice, observe one teacher and record:
    • how the teacher uses language in class,
    • how learners participate,
    • what literacy-support strategies are visible,
    • one thing you would like to adopt in your own teaching.
  • Activity 4: Problem-Solving Task
    In groups, choose one literacy challenge commonly found in Ghanaian classrooms and design three practical ways a teacher can respond to it.
  • Activity 5: Personal Growth Plan
    Write a one-page action plan titled “How I Intend to Become a Good Literacy Teacher.” Identify specific habits, attitudes, and professional practices you want to develop.

6. Reflection Questions

  • What qualities do you think are most important in a good literacy teacher, and why?
  • Why is reflective practice essential for language and literacy teaching?
  • How can teachers support learners who struggle with confidence, comprehension, or participation?
  • What have you learned from observing teachers or interacting with learners in real classroom settings?

7. Application Task

Write a short essay on the topic:

“The Kind of Language and Literacy Teacher I Want to Become.”

In your response, discuss:

  • the qualities of a good literacy teacher,
  • the responsibilities of the language/literacy teacher,
  • the literacy challenges learners face,
  • the steps you will take to become a more effective and reflective teacher.
