Module 5

Bilingual Education in Ghana

A Six-Module Professional Learning Course by Miss Mprah Ruhaimatu

1. Module Overview

This module introduces student teachers to the concept of bilingual education and its relevance to language and literacy development in Ghana. It explains what bilingual education means, the major types of bilingual education, the language situation in Ghana, the language policy of education, and the practical challenges that arise when language policy is implemented in schools.

Ghana is a multilingual country with many indigenous languages existing alongside English, the official language. This means that learners often come to school with different first languages and different levels of exposure to English. For this reason, teachers need a clear understanding of bilingual education so they can support learners meaningfully and respond wisely to the realities of the Ghanaian classroom.

The module also highlights the cognitive, linguistic, psychological, and social importance of bilingual education. Rather than treating bilingualism as a problem, student teachers are encouraged to see it as a resource that can support learning, participation, identity, and literacy development when handled well.

2. Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, student teachers should be able to:

  1. Define and explain the concept of bilingual education.
  2. Identify and discuss the major types of bilingual education.
  3. Explain the language situation in Ghana.
  4. Discuss the language policy of education in Ghana.
  5. Examine the cognitive, linguistic, and psychological importance of bilingual education.
  6. Discuss the practical challenges of implementing language policy in Ghanaian schools.
  7. Suggest realistic ways of addressing those challenges in classroom practice.

3. Module Content

What is Bilingual Education?

Bilingual education refers to the use of two languages in the teaching and learning process. In many cases, it involves the use of a learner’s first language together with a second language for instruction, communication, and literacy development. Bilingual education does not simply mean that two languages exist in a country. Rather, it means the educational system makes purposeful use of two languages in supporting learner development.

In the Ghanaian context, bilingual education is especially relevant because many learners begin school with an indigenous Ghanaian language as their first language, while English becomes the major language of formal schooling. A good understanding of bilingual education helps teachers bridge the learner’s home language and school language more effectively.

Why Bilingual Education Matters

Bilingual education matters because language is central to learning. If children are taught only in a language they do not fully understand, they may struggle with comprehension, participation, and confidence. When the first language is used thoughtfully, it can support meaning-making, concept development, identity formation, and smoother transition into wider academic language use.

Bilingual education is therefore not merely a linguistic arrangement; it is also an educational, social, and psychological support system for learners in multilingual settings.

Types of Bilingual Education

There are different forms of bilingual education, and each type reflects a particular educational goal or language arrangement. The course outline highlights the following major types:

1. Transitional Bilingual Education

Transitional bilingual education uses the learner’s first language in the early stages of schooling but gradually shifts toward the second language, often English, as the main medium of instruction. The goal is to support learners initially in a familiar language while preparing them to function in the wider school language.

This model can help learners adjust to school more confidently, but if the transition is rushed or poorly supported, learners may lose the benefits of strong first-language development.

2. Immersion

In immersion programmes, learners are taught mainly through a second language. The idea is that rich exposure will help them acquire the target language more naturally and efficiently. In some cases, this may support rapid development in the second language, especially where there is strong support, meaningful input, and good teaching.

However, full immersion can be difficult when learners have little prior knowledge of the language of instruction. If not handled carefully, it may lead to confusion, silence, rote learning, or weak comprehension.

3. Submersion

Submersion occurs when learners are placed in a classroom where the language of instruction is a language they do not know well, and little or no support is given through their first language. This is often the most difficult situation for learners because it assumes they can “sink or swim” in the school language.

Submersion is generally not supportive of equity or inclusion because it may disadvantage learners who are still developing understanding in the school language.

4. Maintenance Bilingual Education

Maintenance bilingual education aims to preserve and continue developing the learner’s first language while also introducing and strengthening the second language. This model values bilingualism as a long-term asset and recognises that the first language should not be abandoned simply because a second language is needed for schooling.

This approach is often seen as more supportive of identity, cognitive development, and strong literacy foundations because it treats both languages as educational resources.

The Language Situation in Ghana

Ghana is a multilingual nation with many indigenous languages spoken across different regions and communities. English serves as the official language and the major language of formal education, government, and wider national communication. At the same time, languages such as Akan varieties, Ewe, Ga, Dagbani, Dagaare, Gonja, Nzema, Kasem, and many others are used in homes, communities, markets, worship, and cultural life.

This multilingual reality means that the classroom is rarely linguistically uniform. Learners may come from homes where different languages are spoken, and some classrooms may contain several linguistic backgrounds at the same time. This creates both richness and complexity for teaching.

Difficulties Presented by the Language Situation in Ghana

Ghana’s language diversity is a strength, but it also presents practical challenges for teaching and learning. These include:

  • classrooms with learners from multiple language backgrounds,
  • difficulty selecting one local language for instruction in mixed communities,
  • limited teaching and learning materials in some Ghanaian languages,
  • negative attitudes toward indigenous languages in some schools and homes,
  • teacher uncertainty about when and how to use L1 and English appropriately,
  • mismatch between home language and school language.

These challenges require sensitivity, creativity, and policy awareness from teachers.

Language Planning and Language Policy

Language planning refers to deliberate efforts made by authorities or institutions to guide language use in society or education. Language policy, on the other hand, is the official position or rule regarding which language or languages should be used in particular settings.

In education, language policy is especially important because it determines how learners are taught, which languages are recognised, and how literacy is promoted in multilingual contexts.

The Language Policy of Education in Ghana

The language policy of education in Ghana has changed over time, but it has generally attempted to balance the use of Ghanaian languages and English in schooling. The policy has often supported the use of the learner’s first language or a familiar Ghanaian language in the early years, followed by increasing use of English at later stages.

In principle, this reflects the understanding that children learn better when instruction begins in a language they understand well. In practice, however, implementation has not always been smooth, and schools may interpret or apply the policy differently depending on local realities, teacher preparation, parental attitudes, and available materials.

Cognitive Importance of Bilingual Education

Bilingual education can support cognitive development by helping learners organise knowledge, connect concepts across languages, and think more flexibly. When learners can use a familiar language to understand new ideas, they are often better able to build deeper understanding rather than memorising without meaning.

Bilingual learners may also develop stronger awareness of language structure, comparison, and meaning because they operate across more than one linguistic system.

Linguistic Importance of Bilingual Education

Bilingual education supports language growth by building on what learners already know in their first language while developing competence in a second language. Strong first-language development can support vocabulary growth, comprehension, expression, and transfer of literacy-related skills to additional languages.

In other words, a learner does not have to lose one language in order to gain another. With good support, both languages can contribute to educational development.

Psychological and Social Importance of Bilingual Education

Bilingual education can strengthen learner confidence, identity, and participation. When a child’s home language is recognised in the school environment, the child is more likely to feel valued and included. This supports positive self-image and willingness to engage in learning.

On the social level, bilingual education can also promote cultural continuity, respect for linguistic diversity, and stronger home-school connection.

Challenges of Implementing the Language Policy in Ghana

Although the policy may be good in principle, implementation often faces real barriers. Common challenges include:

  • lack of clarity or consistency in applying the policy,
  • limited teacher preparation in bilingual methods,
  • shortage of teaching materials in local languages,
  • multilingual classrooms where no single local language fits all learners,
  • parental preference for English-only education,
  • social attitudes that see local languages as less prestigious,
  • frequent policy shifts or weak monitoring.

Addressing the Challenges

Teachers and educational authorities can respond to these challenges in several ways:

  • provide clearer teacher guidance on language policy implementation,
  • train teachers in bilingual and multilingual classroom strategies,
  • develop and supply more local-language teaching and learning materials,
  • educate parents on the value of first-language support,
  • use flexible classroom strategies in mixed-language settings,
  • promote positive attitudes toward Ghanaian languages as educational resources.

Why This Module Matters

This module matters because bilingual education is not a distant policy issue; it is a daily classroom reality in Ghana. Teachers who do not understand bilingual education may misjudge learners, mishandle language differences, or rely on approaches that exclude some pupils from full participation.

A teacher who understands bilingual education is better prepared to support comprehension, protect learner identity, and promote literacy more effectively in multilingual classrooms.

4. Ghanaian Classroom Examples

  • Example 1: In a lower primary school in a largely Akan-speaking community, the teacher explains key ideas first in a familiar Ghanaian language before gradually extending the same ideas in English. This reflects a supportive bilingual approach.
  • Example 2: In an urban classroom with learners from different language backgrounds, the teacher cannot rely on one local language for everyone. Instead, the teacher uses visuals, gestures, pair talk, repetition, and simple English to make learning more inclusive.
  • Example 3: Some parents insist that only English should be used in school because they believe Ghanaian languages will weaken academic performance. A well-informed teacher explains that first-language support can strengthen understanding and confidence, especially in the early years.
  • Example 4: A learner participates more confidently when allowed to discuss a task briefly in the home language before reporting in English. This shows how bilingual support can improve classroom participation.

5. Interactive Learning Activities

  • Activity 1: Type Identification
    In pairs, define the four major types of bilingual education and explain one advantage and one challenge of each.
  • Activity 2: Concept Mapping
    In groups, draw a concept map showing the major languages present in Ghana and explain how language diversity affects teaching and learning.
  • Activity 3: Debate
    Debate this motion: “English-only instruction is better than bilingual education in Ghanaian schools.”
  • Activity 4: School Observation Planning
    Prepare five questions you would use during a school visit to find out how teachers implement the language policy of education in practice.
  • Activity 5: Problem-Solving Task
    In groups, identify three challenges of language policy implementation in Ghana and propose realistic classroom-level solutions.

6. Reflection Questions

  • Why is bilingual education important in a multilingual country like Ghana?
  • Which type of bilingual education do you think is most supportive of learners, and why?
  • What are the major difficulties the language situation in Ghana presents for teachers?
  • How can teachers help communities develop more positive attitudes toward Ghanaian languages?

7. Application Task

Write a short essay on the topic:

“Bilingual Education as a Resource for Literacy Development in Ghana.”

In your response, discuss:

  • the meaning of bilingual education,
  • the types of bilingual education,
  • the language situation in Ghana,
  • the language policy of education in Ghana,
  • the benefits and challenges of bilingual education for teachers and learners.
