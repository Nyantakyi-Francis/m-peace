Module 4

First Language (L1) Acquisition

A Six-Module Professional Learning Course by Miss Mprah Ruhaimatu

1. Module Overview

This module introduces student teachers to the process of first language acquisition, often referred to as L1 acquisition. It examines how children gradually develop their first language from infancy through interaction with family, caregivers, and the environment. Understanding this process is important for teachers because the child’s first language forms the foundation for later communication, literacy development, classroom participation, and additional language learning.

The module focuses on the major stages of L1 acquisition and the determinants that influence how children acquire language. Student teachers will learn that children do not begin speaking in full sentences immediately. Rather, they pass through identifiable stages such as the pre-language stage, one-word stage, two-word stage, telegraphic stage, and later multiword stage. The module also discusses key determinants of L1 acquisition, including imitation and correction, parental speech, cognitive development, and inborn knowledge.

By understanding these stages and determinants, student teachers will be better prepared to use language appropriately in the classroom and to support learners from different language backgrounds and developmental levels.

2. Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, student teachers should be able to:

  1. Explain the meaning of first language (L1) acquisition.
  2. Identify and discuss the major stages of L1 acquisition.
  3. Explain how the stages of L1 acquisition differ among learners.
  4. Identify the determinants of L1 acquisition.
  5. Discuss how the determinants influence children’s language growth.
  6. Explain how knowledge of L1 acquisition can shape teachers’ language use in the classroom.
  7. Apply insights from L1 acquisition to support diverse learners more effectively.

3. Module Content

What is First Language (L1) Acquisition?

First language acquisition refers to the natural process through which a child learns the first language spoken in the home or immediate environment. This is the language the child is first exposed to and begins to understand and use for communication. In many Ghanaian homes, this may be Twi, Ewe, Ga, Fante, Dagbani, Gonja, Nzema, Dagaare, or another local language, depending on the community and family background.

L1 acquisition begins very early in life, long before formal schooling. Children listen, observe, imitate, experiment, and gradually develop the ability to produce sounds, words, phrases, and sentences. This process happens naturally, but it is supported by meaningful interaction and a rich language environment.

Why L1 Acquisition Matters for Teachers

Teachers need to understand L1 acquisition because children do not enter school as blank slates. They come with prior language experiences, patterns of communication, and different levels of linguistic development. A teacher who understands how first language develops will be better able to interpret learner behaviour, respond patiently to language differences, and build on what learners already know.

Knowledge of L1 acquisition also helps teachers avoid unrealistic expectations. For example, not all learners of the same age will express themselves in exactly the same way or at the same pace. Differences in language development do not always mean inability; they may reflect variation in exposure, interaction, maturity, and opportunity.

Stages of L1 Acquisition

Children usually pass through several recognisable stages in acquiring their first language. These stages are not rigidly fixed, and children may move through them at different rates. However, the stages provide a useful guide for understanding how language develops over time.

Stage 1: Pre-Language Stage

The pre-language stage occurs before the child begins to use recognisable words. At this stage, the child communicates through crying, cooing, babbling, gestures, facial expression, eye contact, and other non-verbal signals. Although conventional speech has not yet emerged, this stage is very important because the child is already learning how communication works.

The child begins to notice voices, respond to sounds, recognise familiar patterns, and engage in turn-taking behaviours with caregivers. This stage lays the foundation for later language development.

Stage 2: One-Word Stage

In the one-word stage, the child begins to use single words to express complete meanings. A word such as “mama,” “water,” “come,” or “milk” may carry a much fuller message depending on the context. For example, the word “water” may mean “I want water,” “There is water,” or “Give me water.”

At this stage, children rely heavily on context, tone, gesture, and shared understanding with caregivers. Though vocabulary is still limited, this stage marks an important shift from pre-language communication to verbal language use.

Stage 3: Two-Word Stage

At the two-word stage, the child begins to combine words to express more precise meanings. Examples include expressions such as “mummy come,” “want water,” “baby sleep,” or “my ball.” These short combinations show that the child is beginning to understand relationships between words and how meaning can be expanded through combination.

The two-word stage is significant because it reveals the child’s growing awareness of word order, basic grammar, and more intentional communication.

Stage 4: Telegraphic Stage

In the telegraphic stage, the child begins to produce short multiword utterances that resemble simple sentences but often leave out smaller grammatical items such as articles, prepositions, or auxiliary verbs. For example, a child may say, “Daddy go work,” “I want bread,” or “Baby crying now.”

The term “telegraphic” is used because the child’s speech contains the most important content words while leaving out some connecting elements. Even so, the child is showing clear progress in grammar, meaning, and sentence formation.

Stage 5: Later Multiword Stage

At the later multiword stage, the child begins to use longer and more structured utterances. Speech becomes more fluent, vocabulary expands, and grammar grows more complex. The child can now ask questions, narrate simple events, express feelings more clearly, and participate more fully in conversation.

This stage continues to develop as the child enters school and gains more exposure to language in home, community, and educational settings.

Do All Learners Pass Through the Stages in the Same Way?

No. Although the stages provide a general developmental pattern, children differ in how quickly and how smoothly they move through them. Some may speak earlier than others. Some may use gestures for longer periods before verbal language develops strongly. Others may show rapid vocabulary growth but slower sentence development.

These differences may be influenced by the child’s environment, health, family interaction, exposure to language, personality, and developmental readiness. Teachers must therefore understand stages as helpful guides, not as rigid standards for judging all children in the same way.

Determinants of L1 Acquisition

Determinants of L1 acquisition are the factors that influence how children learn their first language. These determinants help explain why language development may vary from one child to another and why both internal and external factors matter in language growth.

Determinant 1: Imitation and Correction

Children often learn language by listening to others and imitating what they hear. Parents, siblings, and caregivers provide examples of pronunciation, vocabulary, sentence patterns, and conversational routines. Through repeated exposure, children begin to reproduce these patterns.

Correction can also play a role, but it is not the only factor. Children do not always learn merely by being corrected. In many cases, they acquire language more effectively through meaningful interaction, repeated exposure, and natural feedback rather than formal correction alone.

Determinant 2: Parental Speech

The way parents and caregivers speak to children strongly influences language acquisition. When adults talk frequently with children, respond to them, name objects, ask questions, tell stories, and encourage interaction, children are exposed to rich language input. This supports vocabulary growth, understanding of sentence structure, and confidence in communication.

Parental speech is not only about quantity but also about quality. Warm, responsive, and meaningful communication tends to support language development more effectively than limited or one-sided talk.

Determinant 3: Cognitive Development

Language acquisition is closely connected to the child’s cognitive growth. As children begin to recognise objects, understand relationships, remember events, and make sense of the world, they are also better able to attach words to meanings and use language more purposefully.

A child’s ability to classify, compare, identify causes, and understand sequence supports language development. This is why language and thinking often grow together.

Determinant 4: Inborn Knowledge

Many scholars argue that human beings are born with a natural capacity for language. This means children are not simply empty containers waiting to be filled with words. They come into the world with an internal ability that helps them recognise language patterns, organise input, and generate meaning from what they hear.

This inborn capacity helps explain why children acquire language so rapidly and why they are able to produce forms they may not have been taught directly.

How Knowledge of L1 Acquisition Should Shape Classroom Language Use

Understanding L1 acquisition should influence how teachers speak, teach, and respond to learners in the classroom. Teachers should recognise that language development is gradual and that learners benefit from clear, meaningful, and supportive language use.

In practice, this means teachers should:

  • use clear and comprehensible language,
  • provide opportunities for learners to listen and speak,
  • avoid mocking learner errors,
  • build on learners’ prior language knowledge,
  • use repetition meaningfully,
  • encourage interaction and participation,
  • be patient with learner differences in language growth.

L1 Acquisition and Inclusive Teaching

In multilingual classrooms, understanding L1 acquisition helps teachers appreciate that learners may come with different first languages and different levels of confidence in using school language. Rather than seeing this as a weakness, teachers should treat it as an educational reality that requires thoughtful support.

Student teachers must therefore learn to use language in ways that support all categories of learners, including shy learners, multilingual learners, and those who need more time to express themselves.

Why This Module Matters

This module matters because first language acquisition is the starting point of later literacy and school learning. Teachers who understand how children acquire their first language are more likely to create supportive classroom environments, interpret learner language more accurately, and respond professionally to developmental differences.

In short, knowledge of L1 acquisition helps teachers become more patient, more informed, and more effective in working with children at different stages of language growth.

4. Ghanaian Classroom Examples

  • Example 1: A KG learner in a Twi-speaking community mostly uses gestures, pointing, babbling, and a few recognisable sounds. This reflects the pre-language stage rather than a lack of intelligence.
  • Example 2: A child says “water” while pointing to a cup. Though it is only one word, the child may actually be communicating a full message such as “I want water.” This shows the one-word stage.
  • Example 3: In a lower primary classroom, one child speaks in longer sentences while another still uses shorter expressions. A thoughtful teacher does not shame the second child but gives more opportunity for interaction, practice, and encouragement.
  • Example 4: A teacher notices that pupils who are regularly spoken to at home tend to have stronger vocabulary and confidence in class discussion. This reflects the importance of parental speech and language-rich environments.

5. Interactive Learning Activities

  • Activity 1: Stage Matching
    Match the following behaviours to the correct stage of L1 acquisition: babbling, single-word expression, two-word combination, short sentence without small grammatical words, longer structured utterance.
  • Activity 2: Group Discussion
    In groups, discuss why children do not all acquire language at the same pace. Identify at least four reasons and relate them to your own experiences or observations.
  • Activity 3: Mini Case Study
    Interview a friend, parent, or relative about what they remember regarding a child’s early speech development. Identify which stages of L1 acquisition can be recognised from the account.
  • Activity 4: Teacher Language Reflection
    Write down five ways a teacher’s classroom language can support learners who are still developing confidence in oral expression.

6. Reflection Questions

  • Why is first language acquisition important for later literacy development?
  • Which stage of L1 acquisition do you think is most significant, and why?
  • How can teachers respond professionally to differences in children’s language development?
  • What role do home and environment play in children’s first language acquisition?

Write a short essay on the topic:

“How Knowledge of L1 Acquisition Can Improve Teaching in the Early Grades.”

In your response, discuss:

  • the meaning of L1 acquisition,
  • the major stages of L1 acquisition,
  • the determinants of L1 acquisition,
  • how a teacher can use this knowledge to support learners in class.
