Module 5

Understanding the Speaking and Listening Component of the Curriculum

Curriculum knowledge becomes professionally powerful when teachers can interpret it, question it, and translate it into coherent teaching. This module develops that curriculum literacy for speaking and listening education.

Academic focus

Curriculum interpretation, alignment, critique, progression, and scheme-of-work design.

Professional emphasis

Moving from curriculum statements to teachable classroom decisions.

Core output

A mini scheme of work plus a curriculum critique informed by learner diversity.

1. Module Overview

Curriculum documents provide direction, but they do not teach by themselves. Teachers must interpret standards, identify progression, clarify expectations, and decide how objectives, activities, resources, and assessment will work together in actual classrooms.

This module helps student teachers understand how speaking and listening are represented in the English curriculum and how curriculum expectations can be translated into schemes of work and classroom lessons. It also introduces critical curriculum review, asking whether the curriculum is inclusive, sufficiently explicit, and responsive to the realities of learners and schools.

2. Learning Outcomes and Essential Questions

By the end of this module, student teachers should be able to:

  1. Interpret the speaking and listening strands of the curriculum accurately.
  2. Identify strengths, gaps, and assumptions in curriculum design.
  3. Translate curriculum expectations into objectives, activities, resources, and assessment.
  4. Explain how progression and sequencing should shape oral-language teaching.
  5. Evaluate curriculum responsiveness to diverse learners and local contexts.

Essential questions

  • What should teachers look for when reading a speaking and listening curriculum?
  • How do curriculum expectations become coherent classroom plans?
  • What should teachers do when a curriculum is thin, vague, or insufficiently inclusive?

3. Expanded Academic Content

What curriculum interpretation means

Curriculum interpretation involves understanding what the curriculum expects learners to know and do, then turning those expectations into teachable classroom experiences. It requires more than reading headlines; it requires professional judgement about sequence, emphasis, and evidence of learning.

Reading curriculum layers

Student teachers should pay attention to standards, indicators, suggested content, assessment guidance, and examples. These layers reveal whether the curriculum values oral interaction, comprehension, pronunciation, presentation, and real-life communication or treats them superficially.

Alignment and progression

Strong curricula show how learning develops over time. Teachers should look for progression from simple to complex tasks, for example from following oral directions to summarising spoken texts, or from short structured responses to extended discussion and presentation.

Curriculum critique

Curricula can be limited by unclear progression, weak assessment guidance, insufficient practice opportunities, or poor recognition of multilingual realities. A competent teacher notices these weaknesses and responds through planning rather than teaching the document mechanically.

Inclusiveness and responsiveness

Teachers should ask whether the curriculum supports shy learners, learners with different language backgrounds, and classrooms with uneven resources. Variety of task types, flexible participation routes, and attention to local context matter if oral learning is to be equitable.

From curriculum to scheme of work

A scheme of work translates curriculum expectations into weeks, topics, objectives, activities, resources, and assessment. It should be realistic, measurable, learner-centred, and responsive to time, class size, and available materials.

Teacher as curriculum interpreter

The teacher is not a passive curriculum follower. The teacher is an informed decision-maker who clarifies weak areas, selects suitable methods, and keeps learner needs at the centre of implementation.

Applied professional question

If a curriculum mentions speaking and listening but provides little guidance, a competent teacher can strengthen implementation through explicit objectives, richer task design, locally relevant materials, and clearer assessment tools. In other words, weak curriculum wording should prompt better planning, not weaker teaching.

4. Guided Learning Activities

  1. Curriculum critique: Analyse an excerpt from the curriculum and identify strengths, omissions, and teaching implications for oral-language learning.
  2. Scheme-of-work drafting: In pairs, prepare a two-week scheme of work for one speaking and listening topic. Include objectives, activities, resources, and assessment.
  3. Inclusiveness audit: Discuss how a curriculum can unintentionally exclude some learners and propose teaching responses that restore fairness.
  4. Progression mapping: Sequence a set of speaking and listening outcomes from simpler to more complex and justify your ordering.

5. Assessment and Evidence of Learning

Short-response prompts

Explain curriculum interpretation, identify one curriculum weakness, and describe what makes a good scheme of work for oral-language teaching.

Applied document analysis

Ask student teachers to annotate a curriculum excerpt and show where objectives, assessment, and classroom practice need clarification.

Planning task

Prepare a mini scheme of work for one speaking and listening unit and explain how it responds to learner diversity.

  • Include at least two weeks of planned learning.
  • Show how objectives align with activities and assessment.
  • Identify one possible weakness in the curriculum and how your planning responds to it.
  • Explain how the plan includes multilingual, shy, or less confident learners.

6. Reflection and Extension

  • Which oral-language demands are made by your curriculum most clearly, and which are implied rather than stated directly?
  • How much freedom should a teacher have when improving on a weak or vague curriculum statement?
  • What planning mistakes happen when teachers copy curriculum wording into schemes of work without interpreting it?
