Module 4

Assessing Learners' Speaking and Listening Skills

Assessment is most useful when it helps teachers make better instructional decisions. This module deepens oral-language assessment by focusing on purpose, evidence, fairness, and the design of practical tools for JHS classrooms.

Academic focus

Validity, reliability, fairness, oral-performance criteria, and assessment tools.

Professional emphasis

Scoring consistently, reducing bias, and giving feedback that leads to improvement.

Core output

A speaking or listening assessment instrument with a rationale for fairness.

1. Module Overview

Oral-language assessment can easily become vague or subjective if teachers rely on general impressions such as "good speaker" or "weak listener." Academic assessment requires clear criteria, multiple forms of evidence, and a conscious effort to separate performance quality from personal bias.

This module helps student teachers understand why speaking and listening should be assessed, what features can be observed, which tools are most useful, and how fair assessment practice can be maintained in multilingual and mixed-ability classrooms. It also challenges student teachers to think beyond marks by using assessment to guide instruction and feedback.

2. Learning Outcomes and Essential Questions

By the end of this module, student teachers should be able to:

  1. Explain diagnostic, formative, and summative purposes of oral-language assessment.
  2. Identify what can be assessed in speaking and listening.
  3. Select suitable tools such as checklists, rubrics, rating scales, and observation notes.
  4. Assess pronunciation, comprehension, interaction, and oral performance fairly.
  5. Recognise threats to fairness such as bias, inconsistent scoring, and unclear criteria.
  6. Design feedback that supports learner improvement rather than discouragement.

Essential questions

  • What makes oral-language assessment valid and fair?
  • How can teachers gather useful evidence when class time is short?
  • How do we assess clarity and comprehension without punishing learners for accent or anxiety?

3. Expanded Academic Content

Why assessment matters

Assessment helps teachers identify strengths, diagnose difficulties, monitor progress, and decide what to reteach. In oral-language teaching, it also makes classroom talk visible by turning participation into observable evidence.

What to assess in speaking

Speaking can be assessed through pronunciation, vocabulary choice, grammar control, fluency, coherence, audience awareness, and interaction skills. The chosen criteria should depend on the task. A storytelling task, for example, requires different evidence from a debate task.

What to assess in listening

Listening assessment may focus on attention, following instructions, identifying main ideas, recalling details, making inferences, taking notes, and responding appropriately. Listening should not be reduced to silence; it must be linked to evidence of understanding.

Assessment tools and methods

Rubrics support consistent scoring, checklists capture the presence or absence of behaviours, rating scales allow performance levels, and anecdotal notes record observations over time. Oral presentations, interviews, role-play, dictation, listen-and-answer tasks, and retelling can all provide evidence.

Fairness, reliability, and bias

Fair assessment requires clear criteria, shared expectations, and opportunities for rehearsal. Reliability improves when teachers use the same standards across learners and tasks. Bias enters when teachers score confidence, accent, or personality instead of the intended skill.

Feedback for improvement

Good oral feedback is specific and actionable. Instead of saying "Speak better," a teacher might say, "Your idea was clear, but pause between points and stress the key words." Feedback should protect dignity while still naming what must improve.

Assessment cycle

Clarify the objective, choose the right task, identify observable evidence, score with criteria, give feedback, and use the results to plan the next lesson. Assessment becomes powerful when it feeds teaching rather than simply ending it.

Criterion What the teacher looks or listens for Possible evidence
Pronunciation and clarity Audibility, intelligibility, stress, and sound production that supports understanding Learner can be understood without frequent repetition
Organisation of ideas Logical order, clear main point, and linked supporting details Response has a beginning, middle, and end
Listening comprehension Accurate recall, relevant response, and ability to follow instructions Learner answers correctly or completes a follow-up task accurately
Interaction Turn-taking, respect for peers, and ability to respond to another speaker's ideas Learner refers to a classmate's point and builds on it

Applied JHS example

A teacher assesses a group discussion about healthy eating. Rather than giving one overall mark, the teacher uses four criteria: clarity of speech, use of topic vocabulary, listening response, and participation. Each learner receives a short note on one strength and one next step.

4. Guided Learning Activities

  1. Rubric design: In groups, create a rubric for a storytelling, oral presentation, or listening-comprehension task. Decide what will count as beginning, developing, and strong performance.
  2. Assessment observation: Watch or listen to a short oral task and score it using a checklist. Compare your results with a partner and discuss why scores may differ.
  3. Bias reflection: Discuss how oral assessment can disadvantage some learners when criteria are unclear or when confidence is mistaken for competence.
  4. Feedback rehearsal: Write or role-play feedback statements that are specific, respectful, and improvement-focused.

5. Assessment and Evidence of Learning

Short analytical checks

Prompts can include: explain the purpose of oral assessment, distinguish between a checklist and a rubric, and describe one challenge in fair oral scoring.

Moderated scoring task

Have student teachers score the same oral sample individually, then discuss differences. This helps them recognise ambiguity in criteria and improve scoring consistency.

Assessment design task

Develop a speaking or listening assessment tool for JHS learners and write a brief rationale explaining how it supports fairness and diverse learners.

  • State the target task and class level.
  • List the criteria and performance descriptors.
  • Explain how the tool reduces subjectivity.
  • Describe what feedback learners would receive after the task.

6. Reflection and Extension

  • Which aspects of speaking and listening are easiest to assess, and which are most difficult to score fairly?
  • How can teachers preserve assessment quality in large classes without overburdening themselves?
  • What steps can a teacher take if two assessors give very different scores to the same oral performance?
