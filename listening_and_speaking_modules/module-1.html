Module 1

Foundations of Teaching Listening and Speaking

This opening module treats oral language as a serious academic domain rather than a supplementary classroom activity. Student teachers examine the conceptual foundations of speaking and listening, the role of oral language in literacy, and the teacher decisions that shape inclusive participation.

Academic focus

Core concepts, literacy links, misconceptions, and instructional foundations.

Best suited for

Orientation lectures, seminar discussion, and the first week of a methods course.

Evidence of learning

Concept analysis, microteaching, and reflective academic writing.

1. Module Overview

Speaking and listening are often treated casually in language classrooms because they seem natural and familiar. In academic study, however, oral language deserves explicit attention because it supports comprehension, vocabulary growth, social interaction, confidence, classroom participation, and later performance in reading and writing.

This module introduces student teachers to the meanings of speaking and listening in education, explains why oral language is central to literacy development, and challenges the assumption that good oral communication simply develops on its own. The module also explores the components of effective speaking and the professional responsibilities of teachers who want to teach oral language with purpose.

2. Learning Outcomes and Essential Questions

By the end of this module, student teachers should be able to:

  1. Define speaking and listening as complementary processes in classroom communication.
  2. Explain the relationship between oral language, literacy, and academic achievement.
  3. Identify misconceptions that prevent effective teaching of oral skills.
  4. Discuss pronunciation, grammar, vocabulary, fluency, and comprehension as interdependent components of speaking.
  5. Describe teacher moves that make oral instruction purposeful, scaffolded, and inclusive.

Essential questions

  • Why does weak oral language often affect performance in reading, writing, and classroom participation?
  • What is the difference between hearing spoken language and listening with understanding?
  • How can teachers create rigorous oral-learning opportunities without excluding shy or multilingual learners?

3. Expanded Academic Content

The ideas below deepen the earlier slide-style content by linking key terms to teaching consequences. These notes can support lecture delivery, seminar discussion, or independent reading.

Oral language as the foundation of literacy

Children learn to use spoken language before they learn to decode print. For that reason, oral language becomes the first arena in which they test ideas, organise thoughts, negotiate meaning, and learn how language works. Learners who cannot easily explain, describe, question, or retell often struggle to produce clear writing and interpret school texts.

What speaking involves

Speaking is not only the production of words. It involves selecting ideas, organising information, choosing vocabulary, adjusting language for audience and purpose, and presenting speech clearly enough for listeners to understand. In classrooms, good speaking also depends on confidence and opportunities to rehearse.

What listening involves

Listening is an active meaning-making process. Learners must hear speech, focus attention, connect new input to prior knowledge, interpret meaning, evaluate important points, and respond appropriately. A quiet learner is not automatically listening, and a talkative learner is not automatically disengaged.

Major components of speaking

Pronunciation helps speech remain intelligible. Grammar provides structure. Vocabulary gives precision. Fluency allows ideas to flow. Comprehension ensures the speaker understands the topic, task, and conversational context. Strong oral instruction develops these elements together rather than in isolation.

Misconceptions that weaken teaching

Common misconceptions include the belief that good speakers are born, that listening is passive, that only fluent English speakers should participate, and that oral tasks waste instructional time. Such beliefs reduce teacher ambition and may silence learners who most need guided practice.

Features of effective oral instruction

Effective instruction includes modelling, structured talk, adequate wait time, clear task purposes, explicit language support, and constructive feedback. It also requires a psychologically safe environment so learners can speak, make errors, improve, and participate without humiliation.

Academic takeaway

Student teachers should leave this module with one major idea: oral language is teachable, observable, and assessable. It should be planned in the same careful way teachers plan reading and writing instruction.

JHS classroom illustration

A teacher introduces a discussion on community clean-up. Before learners speak, the teacher pre-teaches key vocabulary such as litter, drainage, and responsibility. Learners first listen to a short model response, then discuss causes of poor sanitation in pairs, then share one idea with the class.

  • The listening demand is to identify the main idea in the model response.
  • The speaking demand is to explain one community problem using the target vocabulary.
  • The teacher observes pronunciation, sentence organisation, and whether learners respond to one another's ideas.

4. Guided Learning Activities

  1. Concept audit: In groups, define hearing, listening, speaking, and oral instruction. Then compare your definitions and identify where confusion usually arises.
  2. Misconception sorting: Sort a set of statements into three categories: accurate, inaccurate, or partly true. Each group must justify its decisions with classroom examples.
  3. Component clinic: Assign each group one speaking component such as pronunciation, vocabulary, or fluency. The group designs a five-minute classroom routine that develops that component in a JHS lesson.
  4. Transcript analysis: Listen to or read a short classroom dialogue and identify evidence of turn-taking, vocabulary control, grammar, clarity, and comprehension.

5. Assessment and Evidence of Learning

Quick formative checks

Use short written or oral prompts such as: define speaking and listening, explain two misconceptions, and identify three components of speaking with a teaching implication for each.

Performance evidence

Ask learners to conduct a short microteaching routine in pairs. The routine should model one oral-language skill, include one scaffold, and end with a simple check for understanding.

Extended written task

Write an 800-1000 word academic reflection on the prompt: Why should speaking and listening be treated as central literacy skills in JHS education?

  • Define both skills clearly.
  • Explain their relationship to literacy and classroom achievement.
  • Address at least two misconceptions.
  • Use one classroom example to show what effective oral instruction looks like.

6. Reflection and Extension

  • Which learners in a JHS classroom are most likely to be overlooked when oral language is not taught explicitly?
  • How can a teacher distinguish between respectful quietness, confusion, and active listening?
  • Review your course outline and identify where oral language appears directly and where it appears indirectly through other literacy demands.
