Module 6

Planning and Co-Teaching Integrated Speaking and Listening Lessons

This final module brings the course together by asking student teachers to plan, teach, and reflect. It treats lesson planning as intellectual work and co-teaching as a professional practice that improves support, feedback, and reflective growth.

Academic focus

Lesson design, sequencing, differentiation, co-teaching models, and reflective practice.

Professional emphasis

Turning oral-language theory into teachable classroom routines and shared teaching decisions.

Core output

An integrated lesson plan, microteaching performance, and post-lesson reflection.

1. Module Overview

Effective lessons do not happen by accident. In oral-language teaching, planning must account for speaking targets, listening targets, interaction patterns, modelling, timing, scaffolds, assessment, and inclusion. A plan helps teachers stay purposeful while still leaving room for responsive teaching.

This module equips student teachers with the practical ability to design, deliver, and evaluate integrated speaking and listening lessons. It includes lesson structure, backward design, co-teaching models, differentiation, and reflective questions that help teachers improve after instruction.

2. Learning Outcomes and Essential Questions

By the end of this module, student teachers should be able to:

  1. Explain the structure of an integrated speaking and listening lesson plan.
  2. Use backward design to plan from objectives to evidence and activities.
  3. Design oral-language lessons that include scaffolding, interaction, and assessment.
  4. Apply suitable co-teaching models for different lesson purposes.
  5. Reflect critically on lesson delivery and identify practical improvements.

Essential questions

  • What makes an oral-language lesson coherent from beginning to end?
  • How can co-teaching improve learner participation rather than simply divide teacher talk?
  • What evidence should guide reflection after a lesson has been taught?

3. Expanded Academic Content

Why lesson planning matters

A well-designed lesson plan helps the teacher teach with purpose, manage time, sequence learning, and identify what evidence will show that learners made progress. Planning is especially important in oral-language teaching because productive participation rarely emerges from vague instructions alone.

Backward design for oral-language teaching

Backward design starts with the learning objective and asks what evidence will show success. Once the evidence is clear, the teacher selects activities, language supports, and resources that prepare learners to succeed on the target performance.

Lesson components

A strong integrated lesson includes a topic, objectives, prior knowledge, vocabulary or language support, materials, procedures, assessment, and post-lesson reflection. Each part should connect clearly to the speaking and listening demands of the lesson.

Lesson stages

Pre-lesson activities activate prior knowledge and prepare language. During-lesson activities involve modelling, guided participation, pair or group interaction, and monitoring. Post-lesson activities consolidate learning, assess performance, and invite reflection.

Differentiation and support

Teachers can support diverse learners through sentence frames, role cards, mixed-ability grouping, visual prompts, rehearsal time, and alternative response options. Differentiation does not lower expectations; it increases access to the same learning goal.

Co-teaching models

Common models include one-teach-one-support, station teaching, parallel teaching, and team teaching. The best model depends on class size, the task, and the confidence of the co-teachers. Clear role division is essential if co-teaching is to support learners rather than confuse them.

Reflection after teaching

Useful reflection asks what learners actually did, what evidence of learning appeared, where confusion emerged, and what should change next time. Reflection is strongest when it is tied to lesson evidence such as student responses, timing, and observation notes.

Planning checklist

Before teaching, ask: Is the objective clear? Does the task demand both listening and speaking? Have learners been prepared with vocabulary and models? How will I know who has understood? What will I do if participation is uneven?

Applied JHS example

Topic: Giving directions in the community. One teacher models a short dialogue while the second teacher notes key expressions on the board. Learners then work in pairs with a local map. One gives directions and the other follows them. The lesson ends with brief peer feedback and a whole-class reflection on clarity.

4. Guided Learning Activities

  1. Full lesson design: Plan an integrated speaking and listening lesson with objectives, procedures, resources, and assessment.
  2. Co-teaching rehearsal: In pairs, choose a co-teaching model and teach a short oral-language segment while classmates observe role clarity and learner support.
  3. Peer feedback conference: Use a structured observation form to give feedback on pacing, scaffolding, questioning, and inclusion.
  4. Reflection protocol: After microteaching, respond to prompts about what worked, what challenged learners, and what would change in a second attempt.

5. Assessment and Evidence of Learning

Planning checks

Student teachers can be asked to identify the missing element in a lesson plan, improve weak objectives, or justify the sequence of learning activities.

Performance evidence

Microteaching provides rich evidence because it shows whether the plan can actually be delivered, whether instructions are clear, and whether the teacher can support oral participation in real time.

Capstone task

Design and deliver, or microteach, one integrated speaking and listening lesson. Submit the lesson plan and a short reflection on what worked and what could be improved.

  • State the lesson objective and target class.
  • Show how the lesson integrates listening and speaking.
  • Explain any co-teaching model used and why it was chosen.
  • Reflect on learner participation, timing, and next instructional steps.

6. Reflection and Extension

  • What is the most common reason oral-language lessons lose focus during delivery?
  • How can co-teachers divide roles so that both remain instructionally meaningful?
  • When you reflect on a lesson, what evidence matters more than your personal feeling that it went well or badly?
