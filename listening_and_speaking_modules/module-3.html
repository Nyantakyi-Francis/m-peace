Module 3

Technology and the Development of Speaking and Listening Materials

Technology becomes educationally useful only when it serves a clear learning purpose. This module helps student teachers evaluate, design, and adapt technology-supported materials for oral-language teaching in realistic classroom conditions.

Academic focus

Digital TLMs, material design, access, inclusion, and purposeful technology use.

Context lens

Both high-resource and low-resource classrooms are considered throughout the module.

Core output

A technology-supported speaking or listening material with clear adaptation notes.

1. Module Overview

Teaching and learning materials shape what learners can hear, discuss, rehearse, and produce. In speaking and listening lessons, technology can provide authentic voice input, record learner output, increase engagement, and make repetition easier. Yet educational value does not come from technology alone; it comes from the quality of the task built around it.

This module introduces student teachers to the role of technology in designing and using speaking and listening materials. It considers what counts as a TLM, which digital tools are most realistic in schools, how to create simple resources, and how to respond to common barriers such as poor internet access, limited devices, and uneven teacher confidence.

2. Learning Outcomes and Essential Questions

By the end of this module, student teachers should be able to:

  1. Define speaking and listening teaching-learning materials in both digital and non-digital forms.
  2. Identify appropriate technologies for teaching oral language in JHS settings.
  3. Design a simple technology-supported speaking or listening material.
  4. Evaluate the accessibility, relevance, and pedagogical quality of a digital resource.
  5. Explain how to adapt technology-rich ideas for low-resource classrooms.

Essential questions

  • What makes a digital tool educationally useful for oral-language learning?
  • How can a teacher avoid using technology as decoration rather than instruction?
  • What design choices help technology support diverse learners fairly?

3. Expanded Academic Content

What counts as a TLM

Teaching and learning materials include any resources that help learners engage with the lesson objective. For speaking and listening, this may include audio recordings, videos, flashcards, podcasts, projected prompts, dialogue scripts, listening worksheets, slide decks, or recorded learner performances.

Why technology matters

Technology supports replay, pause, modelling, recording, and exposure to authentic voices. It can help learners hear pronunciation repeatedly, compare their speech with a model, and engage with spoken texts that go beyond the teacher's voice alone.

Criteria for selecting tools

A useful tool should align with the objective, be accessible in the local context, minimise distraction, support rather than replace teacher guidance, and remain manageable for learners. Teachers should ask whether the tool improves learning efficiency, clarity, or participation.

Low-tech to high-tech continuum

Student teachers should avoid assuming that "technology" always means internet-heavy platforms. A smartphone voice recorder, downloaded audio file, Bluetooth speaker, or offline slideshow may be more realistic and pedagogically sound than a web-dependent app in many school settings.

Designing digital oral-language materials

Good design begins with the target skill. Teachers then select the input, create the learner task, decide how learners will respond, and identify how evidence of learning will be collected. Age appropriateness, clarity, cultural relevance, and accessibility should guide every stage.

Challenges and responsive solutions

Common problems include unstable electricity, poor connectivity, limited devices, digital inequality, and teacher uncertainty. Practical responses include downloading content in advance, sharing devices in groups, using offline files, blending digital and non-digital resources, and simplifying the task when technology fails.

Digital citizenship and ethics

When learners are recorded or exposed to online materials, teachers must think about privacy, respectful use, and appropriate content. Student work should not be shared casually, and materials should be selected with attention to classroom safety and professional ethics.

Best-practice principle

Technology should be chosen because it improves the quality of listening, speaking, feedback, or access to language input. It should not be used simply because it is available.

Applied JHS example

A teacher records a short voice note about road safety and sends it to a class WhatsApp group before the lesson. In class, learners listen again through a shared speaker and complete a worksheet with three tasks: identify the topic, list two safety rules, and discuss one rule in pairs.

  • Listening target: gist and detail comprehension.
  • Speaking target: restating information and giving one recommendation.
  • Low-resource adaptation: if phones are unavailable, the teacher plays the same audio from one device through a speaker and distributes a printed transcript afterwards.

4. Guided Learning Activities

  1. Tool hunt: List ten tools that could support oral-language teaching. Separate them into low-tech and high-tech categories, then explain which ones are most realistic in your context.
  2. Material design challenge: Create one digital speaking or listening material, identify the target skill, and explain how learners will use it.
  3. Technology debate: Debate the statement: "Technology improves oral-language learning more than traditional methods." Learners must present advantages, cautions, and contextual limitations.
  4. Accessibility audit: Evaluate an existing digital resource for clarity, cultural relevance, learner accessibility, and likely classroom effectiveness.

5. Assessment and Evidence of Learning

Short analytical prompts

Ask student teachers to define TLMs, compare two digital tools, and explain one challenge and one solution related to technology integration.

Practical demonstration

Require each student teacher or group to present a technology-supported oral-language material and demonstrate how it would be used in a short classroom segment.

Design task

Design one technology-supported speaking or listening lesson material and provide a one-page rationale.

  • State the target learners and lesson objective.
  • Describe the technology and the learner task.
  • Explain how the material supports oral-language development.
  • Show how the same idea could be adapted for a low-resource classroom.

6. Reflection and Extension

  • Which digital tools in your context are sustainable enough to be used regularly rather than occasionally?
  • How can teachers ensure that technology-supported tasks still include interaction and not only screen time?
  • What is the most realistic way to prepare for technology failure without losing the lesson objective?
