Module 2

Strategies for Teaching Listening and Speaking

This module moves from theory to method. Student teachers study how oral-language strategies are selected, sequenced, modelled, and adapted so that classroom talk leads to learning instead of unfocused activity.

Academic focus

Strategy selection, dialogic teaching, guided practice, and contextual adaptation.

Professional emphasis

Choosing the right strategy for the right objective and learner group.

Core evidence

Lesson design, strategy comparison, observation, and microteaching.

1. Module Overview

Teaching strategies matter because oral language improves when teachers deliberately structure opportunities for rehearsal, listening, response, and feedback. A strategy is not simply an activity label; it is a method for helping learners achieve a clearly defined outcome.

This module equips student teachers with practical but academically grounded strategies for teaching listening and speaking. It covers storytelling, questioning, discussion, debate, modelling, description tasks, listening comprehension routines, and the use of audio or audio-visual materials. It also asks an important professional question: how should teachers adapt good strategies when classrooms are large, multilingual, or under-resourced?

2. Learning Outcomes and Essential Questions

By the end of this module, student teachers should be able to:

  1. Identify major strategies for teaching speaking and listening in JHS classrooms.
  2. Explain the strengths, limits, and preparation demands of each strategy.
  3. Select suitable strategies for different lesson objectives and learner needs.
  4. Design short oral-language tasks that include modelling, participation, and feedback.
  5. Adapt oral-learning strategies to low-resource and mixed-ability contexts.

Essential questions

  • What makes one speaking strategy more suitable than another for a given objective?
  • How can teachers promote extended student talk without losing classroom focus?
  • What adaptations make oral-language strategies work for shy, multilingual, or less confident learners?

3. Expanded Academic Content

Strategy selection begins with the objective

Teachers should first ask what learners must understand or produce by the end of the lesson. If the goal is sequencing ideas, storytelling may be appropriate. If the goal is justification and reasoning, discussion or debate may be stronger. Good teaching begins with alignment, not novelty.

Storytelling and retelling

Storytelling builds sequencing, fluency, memory, and expressive language. Retelling is especially powerful because it asks learners to listen carefully, identify key events, and reconstruct meaning in their own words. Teachers can scaffold by using pictures, key vocabulary, and story frames.

Open-ended questioning

Open-ended questions move learners beyond one-word responses. They invite explanation, prediction, interpretation, and evaluation. Questions such as "Why do you think that happened?" or "What evidence supports your view?" help teachers hear student reasoning, not just recall.

Discussion, debate, and role-play

These strategies develop turn-taking, justification, attentive listening, and respectful disagreement. They are most effective when teachers provide clear norms, sentence starters, time limits, and defined speaking roles so that participation does not become dominated by a few confident learners.

Listening routines

Listening improves when learners have a task to complete while listening. Effective routines include listen-and-draw, note completion, sequencing, gist listening, listen-and-retell, and follow-the-instruction tasks. These routines shift listening from passive hearing to accountable comprehension.

Description and audio-visual tasks

Description activities support vocabulary growth and detail awareness, while audio or video materials expose learners to pace, tone, accent variation, and authentic speech. Teachers should prepare learners for what to notice before they listen or watch.

Managing classroom constraints

Large classes, limited time, and learner shyness are real constraints, but they do not eliminate the need for oral learning. Teachers can use pair work, rotating spokespeople, short timed responses, group roles, and partial-class reporting to preserve participation even in difficult conditions.

Recommended lesson sequence

Many oral-language lessons improve when teachers follow a sequence: model the task, give guided practice, move into pair or group interaction, monitor language use, and end with a brief debrief on what learners understood and how they communicated.

Applied JHS example

Lesson topic: Should school uniforms be compulsory? The teacher begins by modelling a short opinion statement, then gives learners sentence starters such as "I agree because..." and "A different view is...". Learners first think individually, then discuss in pairs, then participate in a structured class debate.

  • Speaking target: giving reasons and responding respectfully to another view.
  • Listening target: identifying one strong reason from a peer and restating it accurately.
  • Teacher support: language frames, turn-taking rules, and a short reflection at the end.

4. Guided Learning Activities

  1. Strategy match: Match classroom problems such as low participation, weak vocabulary, poor listening, or time pressure to the strategy that would best address them, then justify the match.
  2. Microteaching rotation: Each group teaches one oral-language strategy in five minutes and receives peer feedback on clarity, scaffolding, and learner involvement.
  3. Observation task: Observe a lesson in school or watch a recorded lesson. Identify the strategy used, the evidence of learner engagement, and one improvement that would strengthen oral participation.
  4. Adaptation clinic: Rewrite an activity so it works in a large class, a multilingual class, and a low-resource classroom.

5. Assessment and Evidence of Learning

Short-response checks

Ask student teachers to explain the difference between storytelling and discussion, justify the value of open-ended questions, and identify challenges in teaching speaking and listening.

Performance assessment

Require each student teacher to facilitate a short oral activity that includes a clear objective, modelling, learner interaction, and one method of checking listening or speaking performance.

Applied planning task

Prepare a 20-minute speaking or listening activity for JHS learners and include a brief rationale.

  • State the learning objective.
  • Name the strategy and explain why it fits the objective.
  • Describe how you will scaffold participation.
  • Explain how you will know whether learners listened or spoke effectively.

6. Reflection and Extension

  • Which oral-language strategies are easiest to implement in your context, and which require the most preparation?
  • How can teachers balance structure and spontaneity in discussion-based lessons?
  • What adaptations matter most when learners are hesitant to speak in English?
